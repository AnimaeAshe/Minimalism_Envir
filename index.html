I // OBSERVER EFFECT

[Day Dream] - The Flow

Day Dream draws inspiration from the fragile balance between inner tranquility and external disturbances that I often experience late at night.

I sought to express a somewhat Zen-like concept: the work is designed as a self-generating environment, a state of “Ma”—a serene, flow-like, meditative calm—which is disrupted upon external intervention.

However, a subtle point is that without interaction with the outside world, the self-contained internal state of this environment remains unknown to the outside world and exerts no influence on it.

Disturbance to the environment, paradoxically, becomes a necessary condition for perceiving tranquility. When you attempt to observe tranquility, the act of observation itself has already altered it.

II // FROM STATE TO PROCESS: AN EXPLORATION

[NULL] - The Unfinished Circle

[NULL] is a simple 3-minute digital drawing exercise where viewers are asked to repeatedly trace this perfect circle. But as a human, you can never draw such a perfect image the way a computer can. It was inspired by my own work in digital art—the effort, pain, and anxiety hidden behind the pieces, unseen by others.

With each attempt in this process, the lines gradually fade away. Throughout these three minutes, you'll find yourself endlessly revising, feeling anxious, and compulsively tracing. At the same time, mocking comments from the outside world pile on the pressure.

But I prefer not to offer an explanation; instead, I hope viewers will derive their own emotions from the interaction. Any emotion is valid—some may find it boring, others may find it baffling, but there will also be those who find a valuable sense of resonance within it.

// PROJECT 03 //
VIEW ON ITCH.IO
Overall Review

This work draws inspiration from the widespread verbal abuse in real life that is often “rationalized.”
During the design process, I drew inspiration from the concept in Persuasive Games of using systems to convey an idea, but I aimed to let players experience this emotion directly through interaction.

This work is also influenced by DIY Games and Microgames. The game's structure is very simple and direct:
Move, grab, throw, and fight back—there are no complex progression systems or lengthy narratives. I intended it to be a brief yet intense emotional experience.

Over the course of this semester, my understanding of minimalist interaction and environment has gradually shifted from simply describing a state of existence to expressing it through behavior and interaction.

DayDream uses noise, along with corresponding sound waves and noise patterns, to express abstract psychological states through audiovisual means, thereby transforming them into tangible sensory experiences.

However, this work also has significant flaws due to the immaturity of the concept: it is highly passive and lacks environmental immersion.

Therefore, in NULL, I designed an interactive format that requires active participation from the user, attempting to express the emotional tension I sought to convey in a way that elicits resonance.

The limited drawing mechanism and gradually fading traces transform the interaction into repetitive labor, self-correction, and an unattainable perfection.

[ CLICK TO CONTINUE ]

Finally, in Talk Back, I explored a more direct, aggressive, and cathartic emotional process.

Using the topical issue of workplace bullying as a starting point, I guided participants from merely accepting the designer’s perspective to actively thinking about how to fight back.

Reflecting on the entire semester, I have become increasingly focused on how to design participants’ interactive processes to evoke emotional responses or convey and explore a concept.

Compared to traditional interaction design that prioritizes streamlined, stable, and efficient workflows, the very concept of minimalism—with its inherent limitations and restraint—is better suited to heighten participants’ perception of the work’s underlying philosophy.
